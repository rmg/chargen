I found much to like in the new system. It combines popular elements of classic D&D, collectable card games, and tactical computer adventure games. The focus in the "Player's Handbook" is on the tactical system, and in a good campaign, gamers should still be able to enjoy solving mysteries and finding creative solutions.

I especially welcome the new edition's focus on good-aligned heroes, striving to build a better world, and the many echoes of Arthurian legend, the wellspring of Western heroic fantasy. For the first time, it is easy to incapacitate enemies rather than killing them. Best of all is that alignment is about "trying." If trying to be a decent human being has either been easy for you, or has never made you happy, then your experience of life is different from mine and you must conclude that I am misguided.

I retained the measure of strength on the ability to lift and carry weight from the older edition, at least for now. (I cannot imagine the average human, with strength 10-11, able to move unencumbered carrying 100-110  pounds of equipment.) I predict this will be revised. I also kept the essential equipment list from the 3.5 edition; I'm changing it to match 4th edition as I go. I waived the prohibition on feat bonuses being additive. If this was a mistake, please forgive me and fix your character sheet. I waived the requirements for deadly tricksters. I look forward to some of the problems being worked out -- i.e., as a real-life endurance swimmer, I think fifty-pound plate mail would give more than a -2 penalty.

During my own formative years, many of us saw the global conflict as between Chaotic Good ("Woodstock Nation") and Lawful Evil (the totalitarian regimes). I have retained these options. During the "Dark Ages" -- the default setting for Fourth Edition -- civilization with firm justice was the only hope for a better world. Yet tyranny, slavery, and law as the means of oppression were all-too-real. And even if the utopian dreams of hippies were as impossible as "chaotic good" elves, unicorns, or flying horses, people still cherish the medieval tales of Robin Hood and his benevolent outlaws.
